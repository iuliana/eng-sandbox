# Chapter 2: Mentoring

The first management responsibility is almost always unofficial, you just find yourself in the position of mentoring someone. 

I still remember Adrian. He was younger and eager and wanted to do everything himself. He disregarded my advice even after asking for it. So I ended up resenting him, and feeling like I did not want to answer his questions anymore. Nicoleta Popa was my lead that urged me to have a personal chat with him and tell him how his attitude makes me feel. It went well and I used that experience throughout the years as an example on how to defuse tension and solve conflicts at work.

----

## The Importance of Mentoring to Junior Team Members

The mentor will usually be somebody with some seniority within the company, but not enough for the onboarding process to be too far in the past so they do not remember it. Or the mentor will be a senior engineer that will act as a lead and technical mentor.

* The mentor gets the chance to see what it is like to have responsibility for another person
* and the mentee gets an overseer who is focused on him alone, without other reports clamoring for his mentor’s attention.

* Senior engineers should make time for juniors/trainees.
* Take time to explain the code
* Take time to draw on the white-board
* Make sure they know what needs to be done

----

## Being a mentor

How do you create good, effective mentoring relationships without slowing development down too much?

When **mentoring interns**, make sure they like you. Give them some project to work on. Wthout breaking confidentiality, give them some tasks of yours, explain with them why you need them to do them and how.

**Listen carefully** - core skill as a manager

* people are not good at saying what they mean
