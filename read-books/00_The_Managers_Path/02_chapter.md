## The Importance of Mentoring to Junior Team Members

The mentor will usually be somebody with some seniority within the company, but not enough for the onboarding process to be too far in the past so they do not remember it. Or the mentor will be a senior engineer that will act as a lead and technical mentor.

* The mentor gets the chance to see what it is like to have responsibility for another person
* and the mentee gets an overseer who is focused on him alone, without other reports clamoring for his mentor’s attention.

* Senior engineers should make time for juniors/trainees.
* Take time to explain the code
* Take time to draw on the white-board
* Make sure they know what needs to be done

## Being a mentor

How do you create good, effective mentoring relationships without slowing development down too much?

When **mentoring interns**, make sure they like you. Give them some project to work on. Wthout breaking confidentiality, give them some tasks of yours, explain with them why you need them to do them and how.

### Listen carefully

* people are not good at saying precisely what they mean, in a way that others can understand
    * we are pushing complex ideas through the eye of the needle of  language
    * this is being made even more difficult when people do not share a common vocabulary because of different upbringing, culture and native languages
    * this is also being made difficult because of various disabilities: ADHD, anxiety, OCD all can impact communication
    * language is not something most engineers have mastered in nuance and interpretation
* listening is a precursor to empathy, a core skill as a manager
* listening also means reading body language
   * this is might be difficult with remote working
   * are they looking you in the eyes?
   * are they smiling?
   * are they frowning?
   * are they sighing?
* when you listen and observe carefully, you can figure out if they feel understood
* be prepared to repeat complex ideas a few times, in different way
* basically, shape and adapt your communication style to the person you are talking to
* if you feel like you do not understand what they are saying, ask the same question in different ways and let them correct you
* **you are in a position of huge power in front of your mentee**
    * but am I at Cloudsoft actually? 
    * Cloudsoft is so eager to push the flat hierarchy, that sometimes I feel I do not get the repect I deserve or expect
    * indeed I am in a position of power, I can prevent them from being promoted, but I cannot get them fires, but they can get me fired
    * however, they might be trying to please me, trying hard not to look stupid
    * he or she may not ask questions, even though they do not understand things
    * it is your duty to extract the questions, and make your life easier
    * **the odds of spending all your time answering questions are slim**

### Communicate Clearly

If your mentee lack autonomy, you need to learn to **communicate what needs to happen**. Examples:

* do some research before you ask a question
* show me what you've tried and did not work, let me know if you know why it did not work
* basically assess their initiative and drive

You might need to do some nudging and provide some clarity to make sure they are going in the right direction. 

### Calibrate Your Response

Based on the mentee behaviour and achievements, you need to learn to calibrate your response and actions. 

They might:

* take too much time
* suffer from decision paralysis
* work quick, but the deliverables are sub-par

So learn how often you have to check on them.

Additional: **prepare** for the check-in meetings with them.
Make sure they have the opportunity to show off their work. (Make a presentation about it)

### Ask Questions and Clarify Rules and Processes

Mentoring people that have just joined the company, gives you the occasion to see the company through fresh eyes.

What are the rules: spoken and unspoken ?

Example of unspoken rule: how much you should struggle with something before asking for help.

Bits of process, culture and jargon that are second-nature to me, are completely new to a newcomer. 

Go through the **onboarding** process with them and give the opportunity to correct things that are deprecated. 
Onboarding documents should be kept up to date and giving a new hire the opportunity to update them and improve them 
grows their confidence in themselves and shows them you trust them. Also shows them that they have the power and obligation
to learn and to share what they learn.

When getting a new hire you have the opportunity to introduce them to the human networks, that pass information quickly
and will help them get up to speed faster.

Even if you are an introvert, or somebody that does not find socialization easy, conscious effort and practice in getting
to know new people and helping them succeed will pay off on the long run.
